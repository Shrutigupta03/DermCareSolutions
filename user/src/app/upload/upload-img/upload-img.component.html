Description:
Actinic keratosis (AK), also known as solar keratosis or senile keratosis, is a precancerous skin condition that develops as a result of long-term sun exposure or ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure, such as from tanning beds. These lesions are often considered the earliest stage of skin cancer development and can progress to squamous cell carcinoma if left untreated. Actinic keratosis is most commonly found in fair-skinned individuals who have had significant sun exposure, but it can occur in people of all skin types.

Appearance:
Actinic keratoses typically appear as small, rough, scaly patches or lesions on the skin. They can vary in color, ranging from flesh-colored to reddish-brown, and may be flat or slightly raised. They are often found on sun-exposed areas of the body, such as the face, ears, scalp, neck, chest, backs of hands, forearms, or lips.

Causes:
The primary cause of actinic keratosis is cumulative sun damage, particularly UVB and UVA radiation from the sun. People with a history of frequent sunburns, outdoor occupations, or prolonged sun exposure are at higher risk.
Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common type of skin cancer, accounting for the majority of all skin cancer cases. It typically develops in the basal cells, which are found in the deepest layer of the epidermis, the outermost layer of the skin. Basal cell carcinoma is generally considered a slow-growing and less aggressive form of skin cancer compared to other types, such as melanoma or squamous cell carcinoma. However, it can still cause significant damage if left untreated.

Appearance:
Basal cell carcinoma often appears as a pearly or waxy bump on the skin. It can also resemble a pink or flesh-colored, shiny, or translucent nodule. The lesion may have blood vessels visible on its surface and may occasionally develop into a sore that does not heal.

Location:
Basal cell carcinoma most commonly occurs on sun-exposed areas of the body, such as the face, neck, ears, scalp, shoulders, and back. However, it can also develop in less sun-exposed areas.

Causes:
The primary cause of basal cell carcinoma is long-term exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun or tanning beds. Prolonged UV exposure damages the DNA in skin cells, leading to the development of cancerous cells in the basal layer of the epidermis.

Dermatofibromas are usually harmless and don't require treatment unless they become painful or bothersome.

Appearance:
Dermatofibromas are typically brown or reddish-brown in color, and they may have a slightly darker center. They are usually firm to the touch and feel like a hard lump or nodule. The size of a dermatofibroma can vary but is typically small, ranging from a few millimeters to a centimeter or more in diameter.

Location:

Cause:

Melanoma is a type of skin cancer that originates in melanocytes, the pigment-producing cells in the skin. It is one of the most aggressive forms of skin cancer and has the potential to spread to other parts of the body if not detected and treated early. Melanoma is less common than other skin cancers, such as basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma, but it is much more likely to metastasize (spread to other organs), making early diagnosis and intervention critical.

Apperance:
Melanoma can appear in various ways, but it often presents as a new, unusual, or changing mole. The ABCDE rule can help identify suspicious features:
  • A: Asymmetry (one half of the mole does not match the other).
  • B: Border irregularity (edges are not well-defined).
  • C: Color variation (different colors within the same mole).
  • D: Diameter (greater than 6 millimeters).
  • E: Evolution (changes in size, shape, or other characteristics).
    • However, not all melanomas follow these criteria, and some may have no pigment, making them pink or flesh-colored.
Location:
Melanoma can develop anywhere on the skin, including areas not exposed to the sun. In men, it is most commonly found on the chest and back, while in women, it often occurs on the legs.

Causes:
Melanoma is primarily caused by exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun, tanning beds, or other sources. UV radiation can damage the DNA in melanocytes, leading to the development of cancerous cells.
